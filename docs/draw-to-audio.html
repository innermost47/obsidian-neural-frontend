Overview

Express your musical ideas visually - The Draw-to-Audio feature allows you to sketch patterns, shapes, or abstract concepts that are automatically translated into musical descriptions and generated as audio.

Original Concept by A.D.

This innovative feature bridges the gap between visual creativity and sonic experimentation, allowing you to explore sound design through intuitive visual metaphors.

What Can You Do?

  • Sketch Musical Ideas Visually

    Use drawing tools to express rhythms, melodies, and textures

  • AI Vision Analysis

    Advanced Vision Language Model interprets your drawing using musical vocabulary

  • Instant Audio Generation

    Your visual concept is transformed into a unique sound sample

  • Per-Track/Page Preservation

    Each track and page maintains its own drawing state

Generating Audio from Your Drawing

Transform your visual creation into a unique audio sample with one click.

Generation Process

  • Complete Your Drawing - Make sure you're satisfied with your visual composition
  • Click the "Generate" Button at the bottom of the drawing canvas
  • Generation Locks UI - All other generation buttons (including other tracks' draw buttons) will be disabled during processing
  • Wait for Processing - The button will show "Generating..." status (typically 10-30 seconds)
  • Audio Ready! - The generated sample will be loaded automatically into the track

Screenshot: Canvas during audio generation - "Generating..." status shown

Generation Blocking

While audio is being generated from a drawing, you cannot start other generations (text prompts or other draw-to-audio). This prevents server overload and ensures quality results. Wait for the current generation to complete.

How Draw-to-Audio Works

Behind the scenes: Understanding the AI vision-to-audio pipeline.

1
Image Encoding

Your drawing is converted to Base64-encoded image data (PNG format, 512×512 pixels) and sent to the server along with the current key and BPM settings.

2
Vision Language Model Analysis

A specialized Vision Language Model (VLM) analyzes your drawing using a comprehensive musical vocabulary. It interprets:

  • Drawing tools used (pencil, brush, spray, eraser)
  • Colors and their frequency/timbre associations
  • Spatial composition (top = high freq, bottom = bass)
  • Visual patterns and their rhythmic qualities
  • Stroke intensity and dynamics
3
Musical Description Generation

The VLM outputs a detailed textual description in musical terms (not visual descriptions). This includes:

  • Instrumentation (synths, percussion, pads, etc.)
  • Sonic textures (granular, smooth, aggressive, etc.)
  • Spatial qualities (reverberant, dry, wide, etc.)
  • Dynamic characteristics (quiet, loud, contrasting, etc.)
  • Mood and energy level
4
Audio Generation

The musical description is sent to Stable Audio Open (or your configured audio generation model) along with the key and BPM. The AI generates a unique audio sample that matches your visual concept.

5
Sample Loading

The generated audio is automatically loaded into the track, ready to play, edit, and integrate into your production.

Synesthetic AI Translation

The VLM acts as a "synesthetic AI" - translating visual elements into sonic characteristics. It doesn't just describe what it sees; it interprets the musical intent behind your drawing using a specialized prompt that maps colors to frequencies, tools to timbres, and patterns to rhythms.

Visual-to-Sonic Translation Guide

Understanding how your drawing choices map to musical characteristics.

Important: AI Interpretation Variability

The mappings described below are guidelines encoded in the system prompt given to the Vision Language Model (VLM). However, the VLM's interpretation is not deterministic and can vary significantly between generations.

The generated sound may not always match these mappings exactly. The AI might interpret your drawing differently than expected, focus on unexpected elements, or produce sonic results that don't align with the theoretical color/tool associations described here.

This is part of the experimental nature of the feature - sometimes the "mistakes" or unexpected interpretations lead to the most interesting creative results! We encourage you to share your feedback on how the AI interprets your drawings via our contact page.

Visual Patterns → Rhythmic Qualities

These associations are part of the VLM's system prompt but actual results may vary.

Visual Pattern Intended Rhythmic Interpretation
Repeated marks/strokes Repetitive rhythm, ostinato, loops
Irregular spacing Syncopation, off-beat rhythms, polyrhythms
Vertical lines Staccato, percussive hits
Horizontal flows Sustained notes, drones
Diagonal movement Rising/falling melodic contours
Circular/spiral patterns Cyclical patterns, arpeggios, sequences
Chaotic scribbles Glitchy, randomized, algorithmic sequences

Spatial Composition → Sonic Space

These spatial mappings guide the VLM but are not guaranteed to be reflected in every generation.

Top of Canvas

High frequencies, leads, melodies, bright elements

Middle Area

Mid-range, chords, harmonic content, body

Bottom Area

Bass, sub-bass, low-end foundation

Left/Right Position

Stereo panning, width, spatial placement

Sparse Composition

Minimal, spacious, reverberant, open

Dense Composition

Complex, layered, compressed, full

Stroke Intensity → Dynamics

These dynamic associations are suggested to the VLM but interpretation varies.

  • Light, thin strokes → Quiet (pp/p), delicate, subtle
  • Medium strokes → Moderate (mf), balanced
  • Heavy, bold strokes → Loud (f/ff), aggressive, prominent
  • Varying pressure → Dynamic contrasts, automation, expression

Creative Examples

Practical scenarios showing how different visual approaches translate to sound.

Example Workflows

Example 1: Percussion Pattern

Drawing approach:

  • Use Pencil tool for sharp, precise lines
  • Draw vertical marks at different intervals
  • Mix Black (kick drums) at bottom
  • Add Red (snares) in middle area
  • Use Yellow (hi-hats) at top

Result: "Percussive electronic drum pattern, deep bass kicks, aggressive snare hits, bright hi-hat elements, rhythmic precision, staccato timing"

Example 2: Ambient Soundscape

Drawing approach:

  • Use Spray tool extensively
  • Apply Blue in upper area (ethereal pads)
  • Add Purple spray across canvas (modulation)
  • Light Orange spray at bottom (warm sub-bass)
  • Keep composition sparse

Result: "Ambient electronic soundscape, ethereal blue pads, reverberant space, mysterious purple modulation, warm analog sub-bass, granular spray textures, spacious stereo width"

Example 3: Rising Melody

Drawing approach:

  • Use Brush tool for smooth strokes
  • Draw diagonal lines rising upward
  • Start with Green at bottom-left (organic start)
  • Transition to Yellow at top-right (bright climax)
  • Add flowing curves connecting the diagonal progression

Result: "Rising melodic synth lead, smooth legato phrasing, organic to bright timbral evolution, ascending harmonic progression, flowing movement, dynamic build from mf to f"

Example 4: Glitch/Chaos

Drawing approach:

  • Mix all tools chaotically
  • Use Red and Black aggressively
  • Create random, scattered marks across canvas
  • Heavy spray with varying intensity
  • No clear pattern or structure

Result: "Glitch electronic chaos, distorted aggressive timbres, randomized algorithmic sequences, granular destruction, harsh noise elements, unpredictable dynamics, wide stereo mayhem"

Pro Tips & Best Practices

Maximize your draw-to-audio results with these expert recommendations.

Best Practices

  • Think Sonically While Drawing

    Consider the sound you want to create as you choose colors, tools, and patterns. Let your ears guide your visual choices.

  • Layer Different Elements

    Combine multiple tools and colors to create complex, multi-layered sonic textures. Don't be afraid to experiment!

  • Balance Density

    Too sparse = minimal/ambient. Too dense = chaotic/overwhelming. Find the right balance for your intended mood.

  • Use Vertical Space Intentionally

    Remember: top = high frequencies, middle = mids, bottom = bass. Compose vertically as you would in a frequency spectrum.

  • Iterate and Experiment

    The AI interpretation can be surprising! Try multiple variations of similar drawings to explore different sonic outcomes.

  • Use Pages for Variations

    Each page (A/B/C/D) stores its own drawing. Create related variations across pages for live performance switching.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Pitfall: Too Minimal

A canvas with just one or two strokes may result in very minimal or unexpected sounds. Add more visual information for richer results.

Pitfall: Ignoring Color

Using only one color limits timbral variety. Mix colors to create richer, more interesting sonic palettes.

Pitfall: Expecting Literal Translation

The AI interprets your drawing musically, not visually. Don't expect a "picture" of your drawing in audio form - expect a sonic interpretation of your visual ideas.

Technical Considerations

  • Generation Time: Typically 10-30 seconds depending on server load
  • Internet Required: Draw-to-audio requires active API connection
  • Credits Usage: Same as text-based generation (1 credit per sample)
  • Canvas Preservation: Drawings are saved per-track and per-page in your session
